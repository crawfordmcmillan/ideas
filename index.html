Your regulatory infrastructure has a structural problem

Every time a model improves, an assumption changes, or data gets corrected, you face the same question.

Can we adopt this improvement and still defend every number we've ever reported?

Most institutions can't answer that cleanly. So they compensate.

  • Capital buffers held against governance uncertainty, not actual risk
  • Model improvements delayed because the audit trail can't support the change
  • Manual reconciliation layers bridging systems that should be one architecture
  • Shadow pipelines that nobody fully trusts and everyone depends on

Capital inefficiency becomes the safe choice. But that conservatism has a balance sheet cost.

The root cause is not technical complexity. It is governance complexity. Three requirements pull in different directions: immutability of historical submissions, replayability under alternative conditions, and clear designation of what is official. Most banks handle these through a combination of overwriting history and duplicating pipelines. Both fail at scale.

The versioned execution is the atomic unit of truth. Not a table. Not a pipeline. Not the latest number.

Every model run, every regulatory calculation is stored as an immutable artifact with complete provenance. Once created, it is never overwritten. From this principle, an architecture follows naturally.

Three views of truth. Structurally separated.

View 1

As Run

What actually executed at the time. Historical fact. The exact model version, assumption set, data snapshot, and outputs. Mechanically reproducible, always.

View 2

As Reported

The run formally designated as official for that reporting period. Explicitly selected. Governance-approved. Immutable. Not inferred from timing.

View 3

As Replayed

Counterfactual analysis under alternative assumptions or corrected data. Clearly labelled as non-historical. Never overwrites prior artifacts.

Changes define the future. They do not rewrite the past.
